Jet-based STOL UAV Design
Advisor: Prof. Suresh Sundaram
Place: Artificial Intelligence and Robotics Lab (AIRL), IISc Bangalore

Our aim is to build a UAV with Short Takeoff and Landing (STOL) capabilities that can deliver a 50 kg payload and work efficiently at very high altitudes (5000m+).

Rotors are highly inefficient at high altitudes, therefore, we decided for a jet-based UAV design. The plan is to have a thrust vectoring mechanism which will provide the STOL capabilities.

PHASE 1:

Thrust Stand: For vehicle control, we needed a model for the thrust characteristics of our engine; therefore, I began building a 3-DOF thrust stand and choosing and calibrating temperature, pressure, and force sensors. I produced a 3D model of the stand and carefully picked suitable materials for each part. The manufacturing process proved challenging as I had to learn about the different techniques of 3D printing, laser cutting, CNC machining, milling, and bending. During this process I handled purchase worth over â‚¹10 Lakh ($12k).

Thrust Vectoring: For emparting STOL capabilities to our UAV, we are working on a novel thrust vectoring mechanism. The main concern is to install a mechanism that can achieve the vectoring and does not add significant amounts of weight.

PHASE 2:

Vehicle Prototype: we are trying to design a prototype vehicle as proof of concept of the thrust vectoring mechanism. Through several iterations, I have created a final design that minimizes weight and maximizes distribution symmetry while retaining the simplicity of assembly. Now I am working on developing a controller for the prototype.

Breaking Points of Early Warning Signals
Advisor: Prof. R.I. Sujith
Place: Sujith's Lab, IIT Madras

Rate-induced tipping is the latest addition to the theory of tipping. The theoretical explanations of the mechanisms leading to r-tipping are at the moment unclear. I believe that there are 2 different mechanisms that can lead to r-tipping.

One of them involves relative rate of motion between the system state and a moving fixed point. Basically, the fundamental idea is that if the fixed point is moving fast enough then the distance, in phase space, between the system and the fixed point will keep increasing. Eventually, a time will come when the distance becomes so large that the system gets thrown out of the region of stability.

I have derived a general nth-order set of dynamical equations that describes the relative motion between the system and its fixed point.
